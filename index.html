Open vs Closed Weights Concept

Weights are the learned parameters of a neural network — billions of numbers that encode "knowledge." Whether they're open (downloadable) or closed (API-only) determines who can build on top of them.

🔓 Open Weights
Models like Llama, Mistral, Qwen release their weight files. Anyone can download, modify, fine-tune, or run them locally. This democratizes access but also means the model can be studied, red-teamed, or distilled by competitors.
🔒 Closed Weights
Models like GPT-4, Claude, Gemini keep weights secret. You interact via API only. The provider controls inference, pricing, and safety filters. The model itself is a black box — you can't inspect or modify it.

💡 Why It Matters
Open weights enable the entire downstream ecosystem: local LLMs, fine-tuned specialists, on-device inference, and distillation into smaller models. Closed weights fund frontier research through API revenue but create a dependency on the provider's infrastructure and pricing.

Knowledge Distillation Concept

A teacher model (large, capable, expensive) trains a student model (small, fast, cheap) by sharing not just final answers, but the full probability distribution — the "soft targets" that encode nuanced reasoning.

🎓 How It Works
Instead of training on raw data labels, the student learns to mimic the teacher's output probabilities. If the teacher says "cat: 0.7, dog: 0.2, fox: 0.1", the student learns that subtle distinction — capturing far more information than a hard label of just "cat."

💡 Real-World Impact
DeepSeek-R1 was distilled from a large reasoning model into smaller variants (1.5B to 70B) that rival GPT-4o on math and coding. Llama 3.1 was distilled into tiny on-device models. Without distillation, capable AI would be trapped in data centers.

Quantization Concept

Quantization reduces the precision of weight values. A 32-bit float becomes a 16-bit, 8-bit, or even 4-bit number. Fewer bits = smaller file, faster inference, less memory — but potentially lower accuracy.

📊 The Precision Trade-off
FP32 (32-bit): Full precision, huge files.
FP16/BF16 (16-bit): Half precision, nearly identical quality, 2x smaller.
INT8 (8-bit): 4x smaller, slight quality drop.
INT4 (4-bit): 8x smaller, noticeable but often acceptable quality loss.
1-bit (BitNet): Extreme compression, emerging research.

💡 Why Quantization Shapes the App Layer
A 70B model at FP32 needs 280GB of VRAM — impossible for consumer hardware. At INT4, it fits in 35GB, runnable on a single high-end GPU. At INT8, a 7B model fits on a phone. Quantization is the bridge that makes on-device AI feasible.

The Application Layer Concept

Everything upstream — open/closed weights, distillation, quantization — converges at the application layer. These decisions determine what apps can exist, where they run, who can build them, and what they cost.

🌍 The Capability Cascade
Frontier Lab trains a massive model → Distillation creates efficient variants → Quantization shrinks them for devices → Developers build apps that were previously impossible. Each step in the chain multiplies access while dividing resource requirements.

🧠 The Single Mental Model

Think of AI capability as water flowing downhill. Frontier labs are the mountain spring — they produce the purest, most capable models. Open weights are the decision to let the water flow freely rather than keeping it in a locked reservoir. Distillation is building channels and aqueducts to direct that water where it's needed. Quantization is filtering and compressing the flow so it can reach the smallest villages (your phone, your watch, your thermostat).

The application layer is what grows in the valley below — entirely dependent on whether the water reaches it, how much arrives, and what it costs to access. Every constraint upstream becomes a design constraint for the apps downstream.
