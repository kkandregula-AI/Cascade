Two stages, two different redundancies: distillation removes parameters the task doesn't need (it's training — done once, at high precision), then quantization removes precision the parameters don't need (post-processing — cheap, even per-device). That's why the order is fixed: shrink the count, then shrink the bits.

STAGE 01 / 04

Open vs Closed Weights

A model is its weights — billions of learned numbers. Everything downstream depends on one question: do you get the file, or only an API? Open weights can be inspected, fine-tuned, distilled, quantized, and self-hosted. Closed weights stay in the lab's datacenter; you rent capability per token.

Why it matters downstream: stages 02 and 03 — distillation and quantization by you — are only fully unlocked when weights are open. With closed weights, the lab does the shrinking and you consume the result.

STAGE 02 / 04

Distillation

A huge teacher model trains a small student — not with right/wrong answers, but with its full probability distribution over next tokens. The shape of that distribution ("Lyon is more likely than London here") carries dark knowledge a hard label throws away. Result: a model 10× smaller keeping most of the behaviour.

Feel the difference: hard labels only push "Paris" up. Distillation also teaches the ranking of wrong answers — that Lyon beats London — which is geography knowledge, compressed.

Access matters: the teacher needn't be a frontier model — just meaningfully stronger than the student. But soft-target distillation (what you just did) needs the teacher's full probability outputs, which means running it yourself: open weights, or being the lab that owns it. Behind a closed API you can only imitate sampled text — shallower, and usually fenced off by terms of service.

STAGE 03 / 04

Quantization

Distillation shrinks the number of weights; quantization shrinks the size of each one. Store weights in 8 or 4 bits instead of 32 and the same 8B model needs a quarter of the memory — with surprisingly small quality loss at INT8, and visible rounding at INT4. This is the step that finally makes a model fit in a phone.

Feel it: drag to INT8 — the dots barely leave the curve, yet memory falls 4×. Drag to INT4 and watch the staircase appear. That staircase is the quality trade you're making for a phone-sized model.

Required, or just storage? Neither exactly. On a big cloud GPU it's an optimization — but not only of storage: generating each token streams all weights through the chip, so 4× smaller weights ≈ 4× less data moved ≈ faster, cheaper serving. On constrained hardware it's a prerequisite — no quantization, no model on the phone. And it's applied after training, which is why it sits here, downstream, in the flow.

STAGES 02–03

In Practice

You've felt the concepts. Here's what the artifacts actually look like on disk — and what it really takes to shrink a model yourself. One of these two jobs is a research project. The other is a single command.

Reference · From Download to Device READ-ONLY
model-name/
├── config.json                        architecture blueprint
├── model-00001-of-00004.safetensors   the weights, sharded
├── model-00002-of-00004.safetensors
├── model-00003-of-00004.safetensors
├── model-00004-of-00004.safetensors
├── model.safetensors.index.json       tensor → shard map
├── tokenizer.json                     text ⇄ token IDs
├── tokenizer_config.json              chat template lives here
├── generation_config.json             default sampling params
├── README.md                          the model card
└── LICENSE

Inside a shard: a header listing tensor names, shapes and dtypes, then raw numbers — e.g. model.layers.0.self_attn.q_proj.weight, shape [4096, 4096], bf16. Nothing executable; the format is designed to be safe to load.

What you do not get: the training data, the training code, or the optimizer states. You can run and fine-tune the model — you cannot reproduce it. That gap is exactly why the term is open weights, not open source.

The reality check: most teams never distill. They take an existing small open model and quantize it — because that's one command, while distillation is a research project needing proprietary data. Which is precisely why quantization is the commodity move anyone can make, and distillation on your own data is the part that stays defensible.

# 2 · compress to 4-bit

That's the whole job. One file out, runs on CPU, laptop or phone. Q4_K_M is the usual sweet spot; Q8_0 when you want near-lossless. For GPU serving, AWQ or GPTQ instead — both calibrate against a small sample of real text to choose smarter rounding points rather than rounding blindly.

COROLLARY

When Weights Become a Commodity

The flow has a sting in its tail. Strong open base models are increasingly released free, as commodities — the business sits in the platforms and tooling around them, not in the weights themselves. Which means downloading weights is not a moat: anyone can grab the same file this afternoon. Owning a model became cheap at exactly the moment it stopped being the valuable part.

The One Mental Model

Frontier labs create capability. Open weights release it. Distillation shrinks it. Quantization fits it. The application layer is where whatever survives the flow finally meets a human. And when the weights become free, the moat moves to your data.
